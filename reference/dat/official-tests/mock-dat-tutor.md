**How the DAT Is Structured**

**The DAT is a 4-hour, 15-minute computer-based exam administered year-round at Prometric testing centers. Total seated time including the optional 15-minute tutorial, optional 30-minute break, and optional 15-minute post-test survey is approximately 5 hours and 15 minutes. The exam is broken into four sequential sections, taken in this order:**

### **Section 1 — Survey of the Natural Sciences (SNS)**

### **100 multiple-choice questions in 90 minutes. Three integrated subsections, scored separately AND combined into a Total Science score:**

###    **• Biology — 40 questions (cell biology, molecular biology, genetics, anatomy & physiology, developmental biology, evolution, ecology, taxonomy)**

###    **• General Chemistry — 30 questions (atomic & molecular structure, periodicity, bonding, stoichiometry, gases, liquids/solids, solutions, kinetics, equilibrium, acids/bases, thermodynamics, redox, nuclear)**

###    **• Organic Chemistry — 30 questions (structure & nomenclature, stereochemistry, mechanisms, reactions of major functional groups, spectroscopy, lab techniques). New ADA specifications take effect April 2026 with greater emphasis on mechanism reasoning.**

### 

### **Section 2 — Perceptual Ability Test (PAT)**

### **90 questions in 60 minutes. Six subsections of exactly 15 questions each, always in this fixed order:**

###    **1\. Apertures (Keyholes) — fit a 3D object through a 2D opening**

###    **2\. Top-Front-End (View Recognition) — match orthographic views**

###    **3\. Angle Ranking (Angle Discrimination) — rank four angles smallest to largest**

###    **4\. Hole Punching (Paper Folding) — predict hole pattern after unfolding**

###    **5\. Cube Counting — count cubes with N painted sides**

###    **6\. Pattern Folding (3D Form Development) — fold a 2D net into a 3D solid**

### 

### **Section 3 — Reading Comprehension (RC)**

### **50 questions in 60 minutes, distributed across 3 long passages on scientific or technical topics. Questions test main idea, supporting detail, inference, tone, and the ability to locate information quickly. Passages are dense but require no outside knowledge.**

### 

### **Section 4 — Quantitative Reasoning (QR)**

### **40 questions in 45 minutes (\~67 seconds per question). Algebra, probability and statistics, data analysis, quantitative comparison, word problems, and applied math. An on-screen calculator is provided.**

### 

### **Scoring**

### **As of March 2025, the DAT moved from the legacy 1–30 scale to a 200–600 scale. You receive 8 standard scores: Biology, General Chemistry, Organic Chemistry, Total Science, PAT, Reading Comprehension, Quantitative Reasoning, and Academic Average (the average of the six non-PAT scaled scores). The PAT is reported separately and is not part of Academic Average.**

## **Subsection 1 — Apertures (Keyholes) — Questions 36–50**

## **Format: A 3D object is shown at left. Five 2D outlines (apertures) labeled A–E are shown at right. Choose the aperture that allows the object to pass through completely.**

## **Rules: You may rotate or flip the object before insertion, but once it enters the aperture it cannot rotate or twist. The aperture matches the silhouette of the object from one specific viewing angle. Drawings are to scale.**

### **Worked example (verbal description):**

### **Imagine a 3D object shaped like a cylinder with a square notch cut into one side. From the long-axis view it appears as a rectangle with a small square missing from one edge. From the end view it appears as a circle. The correct aperture must match exactly one of these silhouettes — typically the silhouette that shows the most distinguishing features (the notched rectangle), so trap answers are 'plain rectangle' and 'plain circle.'**

### **Strategy tip: Look for distinguishing features (curves, notches, asymmetries). Eliminate apertures that match only generic features (a plain rectangle could fit dozens of objects).**

### ***Practice resources: Bootcamp PAT, DAT Booster, CrackDAT, and Kaplan PAT all provide hundreds of image-based aperture problems. Drill these daily.***

## **Subsection 2 — Top-Front-End (TFE / View Recognition) — Questions 51–65**

## **Format: Two of the three orthographic views (top, front, end/right) of a 3D object are shown. You must select the third view from four options.**

## **Convention: Solid lines \= visible edges. Dashed lines \= hidden edges. Top view is drawn as if you are looking straight down; front is straight on; end is the right side.**

### **Worked example (verbal description):**

### **If the front view shows a tall rectangle with a horizontal line halfway up (a step) and the top view shows a longer rectangle with a vertical line three-quarters across (another step that's hidden from the front), the end view must reconcile both: a shape with steps on both the height and depth axes. Wrong answers typically reverse a dashed/solid line, omit a hidden edge, or invert the step direction.**

### **Strategy tip: Use the height from the front view, the depth from the top view, and reconstruct the end view edge by edge. Always double-check that hidden edges are dashed.**

## **Subsection 6 — Pattern Folding (3D Form Development) — Questions 111–125**

## **Format: A flat 2D pattern (net) is shown. Identify which of the four 3D solids labeled A–D the net folds into.**

## **Strategy: Pick a single distinguishing feature on the net (a shaded face, an arrow, a unique color), find which face of each 3D solid matches it, then walk around the net checking that adjacent panels remain adjacent in 3D. The exam usually includes shading or arrows specifically as distinguishing markers.**

### **Worked example (verbal description):**

### **If the net is a cross-shape of 6 squares (a standard cube unfolding) with one square shaded gray, one square hatched, and the others blank: the gray and hatched faces are NEVER opposite each other (since they are adjacent in the net, separated only by one fold). Eliminate any 3D answer where they appear on opposite faces of the cube.**

## **\[1\] The human oral cavity hosts one of the most diverse microbial communities in the body, with over 700 known bacterial species coexisting in a thin biofilm called dental plaque. For most of the twentieth century, dentistry treated dental caries — tooth decay — as the work of a single villain: Streptococcus mutans, an acid-producing bacterium that ferments dietary sugars and erodes enamel. The 'specific plaque hypothesis,' formalized in the 1970s, encouraged researchers to seek vaccines and antimicrobials targeting this one organism. Decades of effort, however, have produced no clinically successful vaccine.**

## **\[2\] More recent work, much of it driven by 16S rRNA sequencing, has reframed the problem. Caries appears to result not from the action of a single species but from a shift in the entire microbial community — a phenomenon now called dysbiosis. In a healthy mouth, hundreds of species of streptococci, actinomyces, and others maintain a dynamic equilibrium with the host. The pH of plaque hovers near neutral. When the diet becomes rich in fermentable carbohydrates, however, acidogenic and acid-tolerant species, including but not limited to S. mutans, gain a competitive advantage. The plaque pH drops, and species that thrive at lower pH outcompete those that do not. Over weeks to months, the community composition tilts toward acidogens, the local environment becomes chronically acidic, and demineralization of enamel begins.**

## **\[3\] This 'ecological plaque hypothesis' has clinical implications that the older specific-plaque view did not. If caries is fundamentally a community-level problem, then targeting one organism is unlikely to be effective: removing S. mutans simply opens niches for other acidogens. Instead, prevention strategies should aim to maintain or restore community equilibrium. Fluoride, the most successful caries-prevention agent in history, may work in part by this mechanism. While fluoride incorporates into enamel and improves resistance to acid, it also inhibits bacterial enzymes involved in glycolysis, dampening acid production across many species rather than killing any one of them.**

## **\[4\] Other emerging strategies include arginine-containing toothpastes, which deliver substrate for arginolytic bacteria that produce ammonia and raise plaque pH; probiotic strains of non-acidogenic streptococci that may displace acidogenic competitors; and dietary modification to reduce the frequency of carbohydrate exposure, which is a stronger driver of dysbiosis than total carbohydrate quantity. Each of these strategies treats the microbiome as an ecosystem to be managed, not a target to be eradicated.**

## **\[5\] Skeptics of the ecological model note that S. mutans remains, statistically, the most strongly correlated single organism with active carious lesions. They argue that 'community shift' is a true description but that S. mutans plays a disproportionate causal role in driving the shift. The practical answer may be that both views capture part of the truth: S. mutans is a keystone species whose proliferation accelerates dysbiosis, but eliminating it without addressing the dietary and ecological conditions that favor it will not, by itself, prevent disease.**
